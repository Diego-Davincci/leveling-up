# The Discovery Call: How to Respond When a Client Finishes Describing Their Project

> Notes from 10+ years building on Upwork. This is the moment that separates the developers clients remember from the ones they ghost. When a client stops talking and looks at you, your job is **not to pitch** — it's to prove you understood them and that you have a process.

---

## The Cardinal Rule

**Never lead with price or timeline.**

Quoting on the spot signals two things you don't want it to: that you treat the work as a commodity, and that you haven't thought hard about _their_ specific problem yet. Any number you give before scoping is a number you'll regret.

> Senior people don't quote in the first call. They scope, then quote.

The mental model: **first call = diagnosis, not prescription.** A doctor who prescribes before examining is a quack. Your scoped plan is the examination — and it's also a committed step that filters out tire-kickers.

---

## The 5-Step Structure

### 1. Listen

Understand what they're actually trying to build — and more importantly, _why_. Let them finish. Don't interrupt to show you already know the answer.

### 2. Summarize

Play back the core product in **their** words, not yours.

> "So if I've understood correctly, you're building X, the main pain point today is Y, and the outcome you care about is Z — is that right?"

This single move does more for trust than anything technical you could say. It proves you listened, and it surfaces misunderstandings _early_, before you scope the wrong product. Nine times out of ten they'll correct one small detail — and now you've dodged a costly assumption.

### 3. Question

Ask the sharp questions that reveal you've built this before. Non-technical clients can't evaluate your code, but they can absolutely tell the difference between someone asking pointed questions and someone nodding along.

- Who are the users — internal team, or customer-facing?
- Do you already have users/data, or starting from zero?
- What happens after launch — do you maintain it, or do you need me to?
- Is there a hard deadline driving this, or are we optimizing to get it right?

**On the "fast vs. perfect" question:** never phrase it as fast-or-perfect — clients always answer "both." Frame it as a tradeoff they have to _own_:

> "Are we optimizing to get something in front of users quickly, or is this a system that needs to be rock-solid from day one? That choice shapes how I'd phase the work."

Same question — but now they're making a real decision instead of giving you a feel-good answer.

### 4. Signal + Defer

Mention the stack in **one sentence** as a credibility marker, then move on. Do **not** "finalize" the stack live — that's the same trap as quoting live. The real stack decision belongs _inside_ the scoped plan, where you've thought it through.

> "I'd build this on a modern, well-supported stack I work in daily."

Then set the next step:

> "Rather than guessing a price right now, I'll put together a short scoped plan — the features, the phases, and a milestone breakdown — so we both know exactly what we're building before any code is written. That way you get a real estimate tied to real scope, not a number I pulled out of the air."

This positions you as someone with a _process_, justifies why you're not quoting on the spot, and earns a small commitment that moves the deal forward.

### 5. Close

End every call with a concrete action and a date.

> "I'll have the scoped brief to you by Wednesday — does that work?"

Never let a good call evaporate into "let me think about it." This is what converts a nice conversation into a moving deal.

---

## Handling the "Just Give Me a Number" Push

Some clients will press you for a figure on the call. Don't dodge entirely — anchor a **range** and tie it to the scoping step.

> "Projects like this usually land in the range of [broad range], but I'd want to put real numbers to it after scoping rather than guess. The scoped plan will give us both a precise figure."

A range shows you're not afraid of the money conversation. Deferring the precise number shows discipline.

## TL;DR — The Flow

| #   | Step               | What it does                                                            |
| --- | ------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | **Listen**         | Understand what + why                                                   |
| 2   | **Summarize**      | Play it back in their words → proves you listened                       |
| 3   | **Question**       | Sharp questions → signals seniority                                     |
| 4   | **Signal + Defer** | One-line stack, then "I'll send a scoped plan with milestones + budget" |
| 5   | **Close**          | Pin a concrete next step with a date                                    |

**The one-liner to remember:** _Diagnosis, not prescription. Scope, then quote._
